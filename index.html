The Language

Introducing Sanguis

Sanguis is a self-hosting programming language built on 9 keywords. Instead of objects, you write circuits, self-contained structures that hold their own state, fire when their conditions are met, and emit signals to each other through routes. No classes, no inheritance, no method calls. When you write Sanguis code, it gets transposed into a connectome that settles on the GPU in parallel, in real-time. The compiler itself is written in Sanguis, so the language will eventually host itself.

Structured Like Biology

In Sanguis, programs are built from "bodies", not objects. A body holds its own state and connects to other bodies through "routes", not method calls. When a body's condition is met, it fires and "emits" a signal down the route to the next body. This gives you the ability to write programs that behave like living tissue; each part responds to its neighbors on its own, in no particular order.

Unbounded Data Flow

Data in Sanguis doesn't wait in a single-file line. When your code is transposed into a connectome, the GPU processes every route simultaneously. The sparse skip method "hops" over empty "regions", or groups of warps that hold nothing, rather than reading them as zero. Your programs settle in real-time, and the GPU draws less power than it would processing the same data the old way.

Everyday Hardware Native

You don't need a data center. Sanguis runs on the GPU you already own, the same one running your video games right now. The SDK is 2.2 MB, installs in seconds, and needs nothing else. Your CPU does some very light work to support it, and your current hardware doesn't have to change until you're ready.

The Engine

What is a VANA?

VANA stands for "Voltage Activated Neural Architecture." It is the runtime engine that executes your Sanguis programs. Instead of forcing data through a single processing line, VANA routes it across many paths simultaneously on the GPU you already own. The organism lives on the GPU through what we call the "blood bridge", an async stream that keeps it running in real-time without stopping to sync with the CPU every tick. Your CPU does some very light work, and then gets out of the way.

The Settle

When VANA runs your program, it doesn't step through instructions one at a time. It maps your code onto a "connectome", a web of neurons and the connections between them, and lets the whole thing "settle" on the GPU simultaneously. Each neuron holds a value. Each connection carries a signal. Gates fire when conditions are met. The graph relaxes toward a stable state, and that stable state IS your answer. There's no instruction pointer counting steps. The computation is the settling.

Frequency As Machine Code

Routes in VANA don't call functions. They fire at preset frequencies. Two routes at a 2:1 ratio produce what musicians call an "octave". The GPU "reasons" from these signals by confirming they've converged while staying asynchronous. These frequencies are VANA's machine code; same role as x86 instructions, but in the frequency domain.

Co-Exists, Doesn't Compete

VANA doesn't need exclusive access to your GPU. It runs alongside your operating system, your video games, your browser; everything you already do. The organism takes the GPU resources it needs and leaves the rest alone. When you're ready for dedicated hardware, the same code runs on custom silicon without changes. Your current setup doesn't have to change until you're ready.
